## Introduction
Under what conditions is AI assistance perceived as the more rational decision in comparison to independent problem-solving? In this original study, we observe how perceived task demands, expected performance benefits, and normative constraints interact to shape individuals’ judgments that using their use of using AI assistance is the most rational decision in problem-solving contexts. We define a rational choice in qualitatively as one that is consistent with one’s goals and evidence, and the scope of AI-assistance as strictly LLM-based interactions. <br>

As Large Language Models (LLMs) have become more embedded into everyday life, individuals are increasingly forced to make decisions surrounding the justification of such tools due to the nature of bounded rationality. Bounded rationality, theorized by Herbert Simon, proposes that humans are unable to make fully optimal decisions due to limited cognitive resources such as memory, time, and computational capacity. As a result, individuals rely on heuristics and environmental cues when making decisions. Factors such as the information available, how choices are structured, and how outcomes are framed can shape whether a particular action appears rational.

## Methods & Approach
This study uses a survey-based observational design to investigate when individuals determine that  using AI tools, such as LLMs, is the most rational decision during problem-solving. It observes how perceived task demands, expected benefits, and contextual constraints influence AI use.

Figure 1: Distribution of survey participants by academic standing

A total of 65 participants (N=65) completed the survey. Respondents included college students and recent graduates at different stages of their academic careers. The distribution of participants by academic standing is shown in Figure 1. Participants also represented a range of academic majors, though Cognitive Science and related interdisciplinary or computational fields were most common. Other majors included Data Science, Computer Science, Human Biology, Environmental Systems, and ICAM/Cognitive Science. The concentration of respondents in computational and interdisciplinary programs suggests that many participants likely have prior exposure to AI tools.
