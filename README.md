## Introduction
Under what conditions is AI assistance perceived as the more rational decision in comparison to independent problem-solving? In this original study, we observe how perceived task demands, expected performance benefits, and normative constraints interact to shape individuals’ judgments that using their use of using AI assistance is the most rational decision in problem-solving contexts. We define a rational choice in qualitatively as one that is consistent with one’s goals and evidence, and the scope of AI-assistance as strictly LLM-based interactions.\

As Large Language Models (LLMs) have become more embedded into everyday life, individuals are increasingly forced to make decisions surrounding the justification of such tools due to the nature of bounded rationality. Bounded rationality, theorized by Herbert Simon, proposes that humans are unable to make fully optimal decisions due to limited cognitive resources such as memory, time, and computational capacity. As a result, individuals rely on heuristics and environmental cues when making decisions. Factors such as the information available, how choices are structured, and how outcomes are framed can shape whether a particular action appears rational.
