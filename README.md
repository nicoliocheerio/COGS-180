## Introduction
Large Language Models (LLMs) are becoming increasingly embedded into everyday life, requiring individuals to frequently decide whether to rely on AI assistance or solve problems independently. Because human decision-making is constrained by bounded rationality, these choices often involve weighing limited time, cognitive effort, and available information to determine whether AI use is justified. 
	Bounded rationality, theorized by Herbert Simon, proposes that humans are unable to make fully optimal decisions due to limited cognitive resources such as memory, time, and computational capacity. As a result, individuals rely on heuristics and environmental cues when making decisions. Factors such as the information available, how choices are structured, and how outcomes are framed can shape whether a particular action appears rational. These constraints interact with principles from Expected Utility Theory (EUT), a model describing how humans make rational choices under risk and uncertainty. EUT suggests that people are rational individuals and choose the option that maximizes utility. In the context of problem solving, this may lead individuals to outsource cognitive effort to AI systems when it is perceived to improve outcomes or reduce effort. 
	Under this context, we ask: Under what conditions is AI assistance perceived as the more rational decision in comparison to independent problem-solving? In this original study, we examine how perceived task demands, expected performance benefits, and normative constraints shape individuals’ judgments that using AI assistance is the most rational choice. We define a rational choice qualitatively as one that aligns with an individual’s goals and available evidence, and we limit the scope of AI-assistance to interactions with LLM-based systems.
